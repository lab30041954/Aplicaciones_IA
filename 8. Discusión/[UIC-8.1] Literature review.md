### Literature Review: Occlusal and Proximal Restorations (2026)

#### 1. Introduction and Clinical Significance

Restoring occlusal and proximal surfaces (Class I and Class II cavities) remains the most common procedure in restorative dentistry. The primary challenge lies in re-establishing **proximal contact tightness** to prevent food impaction and periodontal disease, while ensuring **occlusal harmony** to prevent mechanical failure of the restoration or trauma to the temporomandibular joint (TMJ).

#### 2. Longevity and Material Performance

Recent systematic reviews highlight a performance gap between direct and indirect techniques:

* **Direct Composites vs. Amalgam:** While amalgam shows superior median survival (over 16 years compared to ~11 years for composite), modern **nano-hybrid composites** have narrowed this gap through improved wear resistance and reduced polymerization shrinkage.
* **Composite vs. Ceramic (Indirect):** Lithium disilicate restorations demonstrate significantly higher long-term survival (95%+) compared to indirect composites, which often show a sharp decline in success after 7-8 years due to fatigue and marginal degradation.
* **Failure Modes:** The primary cause of failure for occluso-proximal composites remains **secondary caries** and **marginal ditching**, whereas ceramic failures are more often related to **bulk fracture** or debonding.

#### 3. Critical Factors in Proximal Restorations

The success of a proximal restoration is now measured by more than just "survival":

* **Contact Assessment:** Current 2026 studies emphasize a "snap" with dental floss as the gold standard for "appropriate" contact. "Tight" contacts (causing floss shredding) or "Open" contacts are both linked to increased risk of recurrent pathology.
* **Marginal Sealing:** The use of **flowable bulk-fill bases** (as an intermediate layer) has been shown to improve the marginal seal in deep proximal boxes, effectively reducing gap formation.

#### 4. Emerging Trends (2025-2026)

* **Digital Occlusal Mapping:** AI-driven software now predicts stress points on a restoration before it is fabricated, significantly reducing the need for post-insertion occlusal adjustments.
* **Bioactive Materials:** There is a growing shift toward "alkasite" materials and bioactive resins that release remineralizing ions (Calcium, Fluoride), specifically targeted at the vulnerable gingival margins of proximal restorations.

### Amalgam vs composite: longevity and failure

Systematic reviews and meta-analyses show amalgam has lower failure and secondary caries rates than posterior composites for both occlusal and occlusoproximal restorations. [pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/26116767/)

- Moraschini et al. 2015 systematic review/meta-analysis: included RCTs and cohort studies with ≥12-month follow-up comparing occlusal and occlusoproximal amalgam and composite restorations; composites had significantly higher failure and secondary caries, with no difference in fracture rates. [nationalelfservice](https://www.nationalelfservice.net/dentistry/restorative-dentistry/amalgam-has-lower-failure-rate-than-composite-in-restorations/)
- A 2021 meta-analysis (TOJQI) specifically comparing occlusal vs occlusoproximal amalgam and composite posterior restorations reported a risk ratio for overall failure of 0.14 and for secondary caries of 0.15 in favor of amalgam, indicating substantially higher risk with composite. [tojqi](https://www.tojqi.net/journal/article/download/8346/5931/8943)
- A 2025 systematic review on "Longevity of Amalgam Versus Composite Resin Restorations in Posterior Teeth" found median survival times >16 years for amalgam vs about 11 years for composite, with secondary caries the main cause of composite failure and fracture the main cause of amalgam failure. [pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC12378770/)

Typical annual failure rates:
- Amalgam: lower overall annual failure and caries-related failure, especially in high-caries-risk patients. [daneshyari](https://daneshyari.com/article/preview/6053071.pdf)
- Composite: higher risk of secondary caries and marginal defects, particularly in large occlusoproximal cavities and high-stress areas. [tojqi](https://tojqi.net/index.php/journal/article/view/8346)

Despite this, adhesive techniques, conservative cavity design, and aesthetics mean composites are increasingly chosen, especially as amalgam use decreases under regulatory pressure. [pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/40873828/)

### Occlusal vs occlusoproximal (Class I vs Class II)

The presence of a proximal box significantly affects failure risk and mode, especially for composite.

- The amalgam/composite meta-analyses included both purely occlusal and occlusoproximal cavities and showed that composite's higher failure is particularly relevant in more complex occlusoproximal preparations, where moisture control and contact formation are difficult. [tojqi](https://www.tojqi.net/journal/article/download/8346/5931/8943)
- Larger, multi-surface and occlusoproximal cavities in composites show higher rates of secondary caries and marginal breakdown than simpler occlusal restorations. [pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/26116767/)
- Amalgam appears less sensitive to cavity configuration and marginal ridge involvement, although overall survival is still reduced in very extensive restorations. [daneshyari](https://daneshyari.com/article/preview/6053071.pdf)

Clinically this supports more cautious use of direct composite in large occlusoproximal lesions in high-risk patients and the consideration of indirect options.

### Class II composite design and techniques

Within composite Class II restorations, several restorative designs and adjuncts have been studied to improve proximal adaptation and reduce failure.

#### Tunnel vs box-only Class II

A combined in vitro/in vivo study compared tunnel and conventional box-only Class II resin composite restorations under thermomechanical loading and clinical follow-up. [pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/33169273/)

- Annual failure rates in vivo: box-only ≈2.2%, tunnel A 6.1%, tunnel B 1.8%. [pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC8364904/)
- Tunnel preparations had more sufficient proximal contact points than box-only (≈98% vs 84%). [pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/33169273/)
- Using a flowable resin lining as an adaptation layer in tunnel restorations significantly improved outcomes, reducing failure towards box-only levels. [pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/33169273/)

This suggests that tunnel restorations can preserve marginal ridges and still perform acceptably if enhanced by proper materials and technique, but certain tunnel designs (e.g., without adequate lining) may have inferior survival. [pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC8364904/)

### Proximal box elevation and bulk-fill materials

Proximal box elevation (PBE) techniques using bulk-fill composites have been investigated for deep Class II cavities with cervical margins below the CEJ.

- A micro-CT study evaluated interfacial gap formation in Class II cavities with PBE using different bulk-fill composites. [pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC12780275/)
- Low-viscosity bulk-fill and bioactive dual-cure bulk-fill used for PBE showed lower cervical gap formation and better adaptation than sonic-activated and thermoviscous bulk-fills or no elevation. [pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC12780275/)
- These findings support PBE with specific low-viscosity bulk-fill materials to improve cervical adaptation in deep proximal boxes. [pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC12780275/)

Better cervical adaptation is clinically relevant because poor adaptation and gaps at the cervical margin are associated with secondary caries and sensitivity in occlusoproximal composites. [pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/26116767/)

### Clinical implications

From the literature, several points emerge for occlusal and proximal restorations:

- Material selection: Amalgam still offers superior longevity and lower caries-related failure in posterior teeth, particularly for large occlusoproximal restorations, but its use is constrained by aesthetics and regulation. [pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC12378770/)
- Risk assessment: Composites in high-caries-risk or heavy-load patients require strict caries control, optimal isolation, and meticulous technique to mitigate their higher susceptibility to secondary caries and marginal breakdown. [nationalelfservice](https://www.nationalelfservice.net/dentistry/restorative-dentistry/amalgam-has-lower-failure-rate-than-composite-in-restorations/)
- Cavity design: Conservative occlusal and proximal designs that preserve marginal ridges (e.g., appropriately executed tunnels, PBE with suitable bulk-fills) can improve contact, adaptation, and possibly survival. [pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC8364904/)
- Failure diagnosis: In occlusoproximal composites, secondary caries at the gingival margin and contact loss are common, whereas amalgam failures often present as fractures; this difference affects monitoring, repair, and replacement strategies. [pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/40873828/)
